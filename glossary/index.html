Glossary

30 essential stock market terms in plain English

Stock

A stock is a tiny piece of ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a part-owner of that business. If the company grows and becomes more valuable, your stock becomes more valuable too. If the company struggles, your stock loses value. Think of it like buying a slice of a pizza — you own your slice, and if the pizza gets bigger, your slice is worth more.

Share

A share is one single unit of stock. When people say 'I bought 10 shares of Apple,' they mean they bought 10 individual pieces of ownership in Apple. The price you see for a stock (like $283) is the price of one share. You can buy as many or as few shares as you can afford.

Portfolio

Your portfolio is the collection of all the stocks (and other investments) you own. It's like a basket holding all your investments together. A good portfolio contains stocks from different companies and different industries, so if one does badly, the others can make up for it.

Dividend

A dividend is money a company pays you regularly just for owning its stock — like a thank-you gift for being a shareholder. If you own a stock with a 3% dividend yield and invested 10,000 DKK, you'd receive about 300 DKK per year, usually split into quarterly payments. Not all companies pay dividends — fast-growing companies often reinvest all their profits instead.

P/E Ratio

The Price-to-Earnings ratio tells you how much you're paying for each dollar the company earns. If a stock costs 100 DKK and the company earns 10 DKK per share per year, the P/E is 10 — meaning you're paying 10 years' worth of earnings. A lower P/E (under 15) often means the stock is cheaper. A higher P/E (over 30) means it's expensive, usually because investors expect strong future growth.

Market Cap

Market capitalization is the total value of all a company's shares combined — it tells you how big the company is. You calculate it by multiplying the stock price by the number of shares. A company worth over $100 billion (like Apple or Novo Nordisk) is called a 'large cap' and is generally more stable. Smaller companies (under $2 billion) are called 'small caps' and tend to be riskier.

Bull Market

A bull market is when stock prices are rising and investors are optimistic. The term comes from how a bull attacks — thrusting its horns upward. During a bull market, most stocks go up, people feel confident, and it's generally easier to make money. Bull markets can last months or years. The opposite is a bear market.

Bear Market

A bear market is when stock prices fall 20% or more from their peak and investors are pessimistic. The term comes from how a bear attacks — swiping its paws downward. During a bear market, most stocks lose value and fear dominates. Bear markets are temporary — historically they last 9-16 months on average before recovery begins. They can be scary but also create buying opportunities.

Volatility

Volatility measures how much and how quickly a stock's price moves up and down. A stock with high volatility (like 40%+) swings wildly — it might jump 5% one day and drop 4% the next. A stock with low volatility (under 20%) moves gently and predictably. For beginners, low-volatility stocks are usually easier to hold because they don't trigger panic.

ETF

An Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) is a basket of stocks you can buy as a single investment. Instead of buying 30 individual stocks, you buy one ETF that contains all 30. For example, an S&P 500 ETF gives you a tiny piece of the 500 largest US companies in one purchase. ETFs are popular with beginners because they provide instant diversification with minimal effort.

Index Fund

An index fund tracks a specific stock market index — like the S&P 500 or the Danish OMX C25. Instead of trying to pick winning stocks, it simply buys all the stocks in that index. This is called 'passive investing.' Studies show that most professional fund managers fail to beat index funds over the long term, which is why many experts recommend them for beginners.

Blue Chip

A blue-chip stock is a share in a large, well-established, financially stable company with a long history of reliable performance. Think of companies like Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson, or Novo Nordisk — they've been profitable for decades and are considered relatively safe. The term comes from poker, where blue chips are the most valuable. These are the 'boring but reliable' stocks.

Earnings

Earnings (also called profit or net income) is the money a company makes after paying all its expenses. When a company 'reports earnings,' it's telling investors how much profit it made last quarter. Higher earnings usually mean a healthier company. Companies report earnings four times a year, and stock prices often move significantly on these days.

Revenue

Revenue is the total money a company brings in from selling its products or services — before paying any expenses. It's the 'top line' number. A company can have high revenue but low earnings if its costs are high. Growing revenue usually means growing demand for what the company sells, which is a positive sign.

Profit Margin

Profit margin is the percentage of revenue that becomes actual profit after all costs are paid. If a company earns 100 DKK in revenue and keeps 20 DKK as profit, its profit margin is 20%. Higher margins mean the company is efficient at turning sales into money. Companies with margins above 15-20% are generally considered well-run.

Sector

A sector is a group of companies that do similar things. The stock market is divided into sectors like Technology, Healthcare, Energy, Finance, and Consumer Goods. Different sectors perform differently at different times — when tech struggles, healthcare might thrive. That's why spreading your money across multiple sectors (diversification) protects you.

Diversification

Diversification means spreading your money across different stocks, sectors, and countries so that no single bad event can destroy your portfolio. If you put all your money in one stock and it crashes, you lose everything. But if you own 8 stocks across 4 sectors, one crash only hurts a small part. It's the investing equivalent of 'don't put all your eggs in one basket.'

Dollar Cost Averaging

Dollar cost averaging (DCA) means investing the same fixed amount of money at regular intervals — like 500 DKK every month — regardless of whether the market is up or down. When prices drop, your fixed amount buys more shares. When prices rise, your existing shares grow in value. This removes the impossible task of timing the market and reduces the impact of short-term volatility.

Stop Loss

A stop loss is an automatic instruction to sell a stock if its price drops to a certain level. For example, if you buy at 100 DKK and set a stop loss at 85 DKK, your shares automatically sell if the price hits 85 — limiting your loss to 15%. It's a safety net that protects you from catastrophic losses, especially useful if you can't watch the market every day.

Bid Price

The bid price is the highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay for a stock. When you want to sell, you'll get something close to the bid price. It's always slightly lower than the ask price. Think of it as what someone is currently offering to buy the stock for.

Ask Price

The ask price (also called the 'offer') is the lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept for a stock. When you want to buy, you'll pay something close to the ask price. It's always slightly higher than the bid price. The difference between bid and ask is called the spread.

Spread

The spread is the small gap between the bid price (what buyers offer) and the ask price (what sellers want). For popular stocks like Apple, the spread is tiny (a few cents). For smaller, less-traded stocks, the spread can be larger. A smaller spread means it's cheaper to trade that stock. You effectively 'pay' the spread every time you buy or sell.

Broker

A broker is a company that lets you buy and sell stocks. In Denmark, popular brokers include Saxo Bank, Nordnet, and Lunar Invest. They provide the platform (app or website) where you place orders, and they execute those trades on the stock exchange for you. Some charge fees per trade, while others offer commission-free trading.

Ticker

A ticker symbol is the short abbreviation used to identify a stock on an exchange. Apple's ticker is AAPL, Microsoft is MSFT, and Novo Nordisk on the Copenhagen exchange is NOVO-B.CO. When you search for a stock or see it mentioned in news, the ticker is the quickest way to identify exactly which company is being discussed.

IPO

An IPO (Initial Public Offering) is when a private company sells shares to the public for the first time — it 'goes public.' Before an IPO, you can't buy the stock. After an IPO, anyone can buy shares on the stock exchange. IPOs can be exciting but also risky — the price often swings wildly in the first days and months as the market figures out what the company is worth.

Market Order

A market order is an instruction to buy or sell a stock immediately at whatever the current price is. It guarantees your trade will happen, but not exactly at what price (it might shift slightly between when you click 'buy' and when the trade executes). For most beginners buying popular stocks, market orders work fine.

Limit Order

A limit order is an instruction to buy or sell only at a specific price or better. For example, 'buy Apple only if the price drops to $275.' If the price never reaches your limit, the trade never happens. Limit orders give you more control but require patience. They're useful when you want to buy a stock but only if it drops to a price you consider fair.

Yield

Yield is the income you earn from an investment expressed as a percentage of its price. For stocks, it usually refers to dividend yield — the annual dividend divided by the stock price. A stock priced at 100 DKK that pays 3 DKK in yearly dividends has a 3% yield. Higher yield means more income, but very high yields (above 7-8%) can signal that a dividend might not be sustainable.

Compound Interest

Compound interest (or compound growth) is when your investment earnings generate their own earnings. If you invest 10,000 DKK and earn 8% (800 DKK), next year you earn 8% on 10,800 DKK (864 DKK), and so on. Over decades, this snowball effect is enormous — Einstein reportedly called it the 'eighth wonder of the world.' This is why starting early matters so much.

Risk Tolerance

Risk tolerance is how much loss you can handle — both financially and emotionally — without panicking and making bad decisions. A person with high risk tolerance can watch their portfolio drop 30% and stay calm. Someone with low risk tolerance might panic at a 10% drop. Knowing your risk tolerance helps you choose stocks that let you sleep at night. There's no shame in being conservative — protecting your money is always smart.
