## 1. What we are building
PacificLink-1 is a new submarine fibre optic cable system connecting eight markets
across Asia and the Western Pacific. It is a high-capacity backbone for cloud, mobile
backhaul and international transit. The programme is worth approximately USD 1.3 billion
and is planned over 60 months from contract award to ready-for-service.

The main trunk route is about 9,500 km, with branching units to each landing country.
The trunk carries 24 fibre pairs with an initial design capacity of around 18 Tbps per
fibre pair, using coherent transmission and ROADM branching.

## 2. The eight landing countries
The cable comes ashore in eight countries. Each landing needs a cable landing station,
a beach manhole, terrestrial backhaul to a major data centre, and that country's
national cable-landing licence:

- **Indonesia** — landing near Batam, backhaul to Jakarta.
- **Malaysia** — landing near Mersing, backhaul to Kuala Lumpur.
- **Brunei** — landing near Tutong, backhaul to Bandar Seri Begawan.
- **Vietnam** — landing near Da Nang, backhaul to Ho Chi Minh City.
- **Thailand** — landing near Songkhla, backhaul to Bangkok.
- **Philippines** — landing near Batangas, backhaul to Manila.
- **Taiwan** — landing near Tamsui (Taipei), interconnect to the Taipei metro.
- **Guam** — landing near Piti, interconnect to the Pacific hub.

## 3. Scope of work
- Marine route survey, desktop study and permitting across all eight territories.
- Submarine cable and repeater supply, marine installation and shallow-water burial.
- Cable landing stations: civil works, power and DC plant, SLTE and network management.
- Terrestrial backhaul to each anchor data centre.
- System integration, commissioning and end-to-end testing (OTDR, BER, capacity)
  through to ready-for-service acceptance.

## 4. Main constraints and risks
- Multi-country permitting and cable-landing licences sit on the critical path.
- Marine weather: typhoon season around the Philippines, Taiwan and Guam; monsoon in
  the Indonesia, Malaysia and Brunei waters.
- Territorial sensitivities in the South China Sea affecting route and survey work.
- Limited cable-ship availability and long-lead repeaters and trunk cable.
- Environmental approvals near coral reefs and protected coastal zones.

## 5. Stakeholders
- Owning consortium (investors and anchor customers).
- National telecom regulators and landing-party operators in each country.
- Marine installation contractor and submarine cable supplier.
- Lenders' technical advisor and an independent environmental consultant.

## 6. What "done" looks like
- All eight cable-landing licences secured and all eight stations accepted.
- End-to-end system tested and accepted at the contracted capacity.
- Handover to operations with as-built documentation and spares.
- Delivered within the USD 1.3 billion budget and the 60-month schedule.
